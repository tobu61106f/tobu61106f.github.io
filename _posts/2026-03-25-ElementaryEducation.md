I've seen a lot of discussion about "Gaokao PTSD" on Weibo and Twitter over the years — dreaming years after graduation that you're sitting in the exam hall, unable to solve anything. I can't claim I'm immune. In 2023 and 2024, I'd sometimes dream I was back in elementary school doing graduate-level math — something like solving ODEs via Vector Spaces — and wake up in a cold sweat:

> "What the f**k, how am I solving homogeneous linear systems in elementary school?"

That's just one symptom. And among first-generation and 1.5-generation immigrants like me, I'm hardly a rare case.

For a long time, I rarely talked about this openly — my distaste for those years, for the Chinese education system. I told myself the damage was negligible. But after years of living in America and comparing it to what I'd call "surviving" in China, I've come to think the system does two things: it produces obedient, clever useful idiots, and it produces what the West would recognize as the far-right. Both start in elementary school.

What kind of education did we actually get? Honestly, my memories of it are a mess. Why were we learning English? Why math? Why memorize all that classical Chinese? Go back to first grade — can you answer any of those questions?
I couldn't.

Some people say "I learned it to make money someday." Fine. I had no idea why I was learning English in first grade. When I told American classmates about it later, their first reaction was "that's insane." I scored a 62 on my first English exam. I had no idea what I was learning, no idea why the teacher was screaming at me. None of that stopped me from finishing a B.S. in the U.S. and working on a master's now.

Not knowing isn't the problem. The problem is when the answer to "why?" is: there is no why, asking too many questions will only cause you trouble. At that point you're not being educated. You're being tested for compliance. That's not a flaw in the system — that's the system working exactly as designed.

What were the girls doing back then? Maturing earlier, acting out, "bullying" boys — sure, that happens in American elementary schools too. But did the boys in China get any education about gender relations? No. Did anyone — boys or girls — get any education about love, death, equality? Also no. Without a family open-minded enough to fill that gap, both sides can walk away from those years resenting the other's entire gender. That's not an accident. That's the precondition for misogyny, for misandry, for the gender hatred you see everywhere now.

Still not there? Keep reading.

If you go back and read Chinese elementary history and social studies textbooks — and compare them to what they use in the U.S., Japan, South Korea — you'll notice something has been quietly removed: any description of democracy from the perspective of the individual. Grassroots elections, starting from the community level, were never going to appear. Using Japan as a frame: the concept of local self-governance (地方自治体) has been cut out entirely, and in its place you get "People's Democratic Dictatorship" (人民民主专政), dressed up to sound like the same thing.
Concretely: What is a constitution? What does it do? Can you eat it? Where did your right to vote go? Your right to run for office? Your right to representation? The role of local representatives — at the village, town, neighborhood level — is never explained. Anything resembling an upper house, lower house, a parliament — it simply never gets to sit at the table.

You might say: what human rights does an elementary schooler have? Why write any of this?

Because this is exactly the level at which certain things get defined — or don't. What love is. What our community is. Where power comes from and who it answers to. Human rights, civil rights, the right to vote, to be elected, to belong to something, to hold power accountable. Leave these blank in childhood and they stay blank.

Call me a democracy crusader (民逗) if you want — I've never tried to hide it, and you're welcome to stigmatize me however you like. But without those foundations, what you get is the assembly-line production of Little Pinks (小粉红) — and the fact that their contempt for life, property, and political power lines up so neatly with their hostility toward women is not a coincidence. Little Pinks and radical separatist feminists are, in one key way, identical: neither has any concept of how power actually flows. Little Pinks fantasize about allying with Russia to bring down American hegemony and somehow becoming first-class citizens of the world. Separatist feminists fantasize about physically isolating men and ruling over them. Neither has anything to do with the suffrage movements in the U.S. or U.K.

The boys aren't doing better. When you climb out of one rabbit hole you fall into another. Their move has been to meme-ify Dong Zhimin — that farmer from the flood plains — to gross out their peers: "should've been chained up" (欠栓了), "should've been dealt with long ago." But both camps are equally blind to how power works. Most of them self-identify as STEM guys, second-tier university students, making ¥3,500 a month, 3D people. The gender war you're watching is mostly these two groups tearing each other apart.

My point: the gender hatred of my generation was already germinating in elementary school.

Why bring all this up now? This morning I had coffee, opened X, and came across a survey on Japanese public attitudes toward the war. The numbers looked like this:

> "It was a war of Japanese aggression."
> National average: 35% / Middle and high school students: 30%
 
>"It was unavoidable for a resource-poor Japan."
> National average: 13% / Middle and high school students: 22%
> 
> "Japan alone shouldn't have to reflect."
> National average: 28% / Middle and high school students: 38%

For me, its not surprising. Japanese textbooks spend roughly a quarter of their coverage on war crimes committed on the Chinese mainland, another quarter on the annexation of Korea (韓国併合), and the remaining half on the carpet-bombing of the Keihanshin corridor, the Tokyo metro area, and Nagoya by U.S. forces. The result is that the victim side gets amplified while the cause gets buried.

There's something that doesn't sit right with me about treating soldiers marched to the front at gunpoint as people who simply experienced the war as a kind of natural disaster. I felt it last summer when I saw a JCP-affiliated group at a tram stop near 原爆ドーム前 in Hiroshima running an atomic bomb exhibit. Maybe it goes back to the GHQ — which thought it was fair, eighty years ago, to equate the bells of Nagasaki with the massacre in Manila.

Memories of the mainland air raids, the kamikaze missions, the Battle of Imphal — these are broadly shared among Japanese people. But if the events that caused them aren't equally shared, then "we must never repeat war" is just noise.

This is what makes the generational trend in those numbers troubling — not the figures alone, but the direction. On "Japan alone shouldn't have to reflect," younger respondents scored consistently higher than the national average. This isn't memory naturally fading. It's a structural gap in understanding, being reproduced on schedule, and eventually taking a concrete political shape.

In the July 20, 2025 House of Councillors election, Sanseito received 7.43 million proportional votes — 12.88% — and became the third-largest party in the country, behind only the LDP and the DPP. A party that entered the Diet with a single seat in 2022 now has enough seats to submit non-budgetary legislation. Under three years.

They ran on "Japanese First," built their base through social media, and positioned themselves as reformists against elite politics — which pulled younger voters who were already done with the LDP establishment. Their rise isn't purely about xenophobia. It's also distrust of the old order, looking for somewhere to land. It just landed on the simplest available target: outsiders. And feminists.

The civic education gap isn't that people don't know what elections are. It's that they were never taught where power comes from, who it answers to, or how the boundaries of "our community" get drawn. Leave those questions blank and they fill themselves — usually with whatever's most emotionally immediate. Us and them. Japanese and foreign.

On the LDP side, Takaichi Sanae ran her leadership campaign playing the anti-immigration card in Trump's register, chanting "Japan Is Back." When a fringe party successfully defines the terms of debate, the establishment has no choice but to follow them rightward — responding to the same anxieties with a more polished version of the same dog-whistle.

The Economist called Takaichi a hardline nationalist security hawk. In her leadership debate, she positioned herself as a "Nara woman" who "worries about the deer in Nara Park," accusing foreign tourists of destroying what Japanese people hold precious. Nara park officials said they'd received no such reports — but that's not the point. The point was never the deer. It was the emotion underneath: the narrative that a "pure Japan" is being defiled by outside forces. That's how dog-whistle politics works. You float a specific, deniable detail. Underneath it, you're activating something much larger.

All of this requires one precondition: an audience that was never taught to ask what the limits of power are, who gets to define the community, what they themselves can do in the political process. Erase those questions from the textbook in elementary school, and what you're left with is a vacuum — available for whoever shows up first with an emotionally satisfying answer. Sanseito and Takaichi just happened to show up with theirs.

The victim narrative of history education and the systemic absence of civic education converge here. A voter who doesn't know whom to hold accountable and a voter who's been told foreigners are the problem will often make the same choice at the ballot box. That's not coincidence. It's the logical extension of a design — even if the people who built it didn't fully intend it.

-----

If the education system's hollowness is a chronic illness, what I've been watching in my native-language community is what the final symptoms look like.

Among my generation, a shared consensus no longer exists. I can't find a clean example from someone else's life to illustrate this, so I'll use my own.

I remember when the Simplified Chinese information structure held the whole society together around something — I'll call it the "CCTV News Broadcast Consensus." Not that everyone agreed with the content. But at least everyone knew what everyone else was talking about. Anyone who was on Weibo in those years knows how wide the scope of discussion was — even if you disagreed with everything being said, you knew one thing: you knew that I knew, and I knew that you knew. It was crude, it bore the marks of surveillance and control, but it was public. There was a shared floor.

I was in high school then, too young, and I hated it. It felt like a kind of oppression. I imagined something else — modeled vaguely on how I understood the Japanese media environment — where people would just read actual news coverage, form their own judgments, say what they thought in a coherent way. A rational, open discussion culture.

That fantasy came true. Just not how I imagined it.

The consensus being broke these years. But what replaced it wasn't rational discussion, it was total humilinate fragmentation. Now when I open a Simplified Chinese webpage I genuinely can't tell what people are saying or what they want. Not because of a language gap — I grew up with this language. It's because those words aren't trying to communicate anything anymore. They've become a sorting mechanism: a set of in-group signals that outsiders can't parse, used to draw lines between us and them, and then breed hatred and mutual attacks inside those lines. A particularly clear example of this is what happened to "gender  discourse" — or rather, what it became.

Everyone's lying in bed scrolling short videos, certain they've grasped the complete truth of the world. No need for discussion. No need for argument. Just enough enemies to confirm they're right.

This is what civic illiteracy actually looks like when it finishes cooking. When no one has been taught who power answers to, what a community is, what they themselves could do — when history gets reduced to victimhood, gender gets reduced to warfare, politics gets reduced to picking sides — language stops being for communication. It becomes a weapon for mobilization and exclusion.

What's playing out in Japanese politics between Sanseito and Takaichi, and what's happening inside the Simplified Chinese information bubble, are the same mechanism running in different contexts: a person who was never taught how to think about power will eventually have someone else decide for them who to hate.
This isn't one generation's moral failure. It's what you get when a system runs long enough without correction.

What makes me pessimistic isn't the current state — it's the inertia. When the gibberish has become a social dialect, when brewing mutual hatred has become entertainment, the cost of fixing it will be far higher than the cost of planting the seed in the first place. We are paying, as a society, for a few pages that were quietly removed from certain textbooks decades ago.

-----
I already felt wrong about everything around me back in elementary school, years before I left. Later, I caught the last train out before the immigration window closed, and move to United States, on this point on myself I felt I'm lucky.

At the most fundamental level, I have no interest in politics. But toward systems that push people to the margins — that corner them and leave them no way out — I have a quiet, persistent anger that doesn't really go away.

At American universities, most students drift further left, regardless of gender. That doesn't surprise me. At American universities, what you'd actually call gender hatred barely exists as a phenomenon.

My elementary school PTSD doesn't end there. I had a terrible homeroom teacher. A terrible math teacher. My English teacher was decent enough, which is probably why I held on through middle school before leaving.

In the 3,650 days or so since then, I've often tried to imagine what my adolescence would have looked like if I'd stayed. Honestly, it's hard to picture. But looking at how things have gone — I suspect I'd have ended up at a second-tier university, become a miserable incel, and be delivering food on an e-bike right now. I won't rule out the other possibility: maybe I'd have gotten into a 985 or 211 school and still ended up delivering food — but the outcome might have been different in one way. Once the seed of doubt gets planted, when it stops growing is no longer entirely up to you.

Science without the humanities is ignorance. The humanities without science are dangerous. If the system doesn't correct itself in time, then when my generation comes to power, things will not go well for China or for Chinese people. I have no doubt that what we're living through is a Weimar moment, a Taisho Democracy — and that what follows may be a very bloody storm, turned inward.
